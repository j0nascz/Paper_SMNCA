\section{Community Detection Methods}
In this section, we will explore the community detection methods that form
the core of this study.
\subsection{Edge Betweenness}
The edge betweenness method, introduced by Girvan and Newman (2002),
was made to sidestep the shortcomings of traditional community detection methods like 
the hierarchical clustering approach. The hierarchical clustering method focuses on 
trying to construct a measure that tells us which edges are most central to communities.
Using these measures, it constructs communities by adding the strongest edges
to an initially empty graph. However, this method has several drawbacks,
such as the fact that it relies on a subjective choice of the similarity measure.
Furthermore, because it tries to build communities by adding edges,
early mistakes made by noise in the data cannot be corrected later on.
The edge betweenness method, on the other hand, removed weak edges from the 
original graph, instead of adding edges.
To find the weak edges of a graoh, Girvan and Newman generalized Freeman's betweenness centrality
to edges.
\textcite{newmanandgirvan2002} states if a network contains communities
or groups that are only loosely connected by fe integroup edges, then all shortest paths
different communities must go along one of these few edges. Thus, the edges connecting
communities will have high edge betweenness. By removing these edges, we seperate groups
from one another and so reveal the underlying community structure of the graph.
The algorithm they propose is simply stated as follows:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item Compute the edge betweenness for all edges in the network.
  \item Remove the edge with the highest edge betweenness.
  \item Recalculate the edge betweenness for all edges affected by the removal.
  \item Repeat from step 2 until no edges remain.
\end{enumerate}
The iteration ends when no edges remain, so in praxis, we will have
to decide when to stop the algorithm. This can be done by looking at the modularity of
the network after each iteration and stopping when the modularity is maximized.
This algorithm is computationally expensive, because we have
to recalculate the edge betweenness after each iteration. Making the algorithm
runs in worst-case time $O(m^2n)$, where $m$ is the number of edges and $n$ is 
the number of nodes in the graph.
 So it is tempting to calculate the betweenness of all edges only once and then remove them in order
of decreasing betweenness. However, we find that this approach does not work well,
because if two communities are connected by more than one edge, then there is 
no guarantee that all of the edges will have high betweenness. So by
recalculating betweennesses after the removal of each edge, we can ensure that 
at least one of the remaining edges between two communities will always have high betweenness.

\subsection{Louvain Method}
The Louvain Algorithm introduced by Blondel et al. (2008) is a popular method
for community detection in large networks. The core idea is to group nodes 
so that connections inside communities are denser, than expected at random.
Louvain does this by greedy, local optmization of the modularity measure, 
combined with a hierarchical approach.
The algorithm consists of two phases that are repeated iteratively until no
further improvement in modularity is possible:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Modularity Optimization:} 
  \begin{itemize}
    \item Initially, each node is assigned to its own community. 
    \item For each node i, we consider the moving it to the community of each neighbor.
    \item The change in modularity $\Delta Q$ is calculated for each possible move, 
    and the node is moved to the community that results in the highest increase in modularity.
    $\Delta Q$ is given by the formula: 
    \[\Delta Q=Q(i \in C)-Q(i \in A)\]
    where $Q(i \in C)$ is the modularity of the network if node i is moved to community C, 
    and $Q(i \in A)$ is the modularity if node i remains in its current community A.
    The formula for calculating modularity is as earlier given by: 
    \[Q=\frac{1}{2m}\sum_{ij}\left[A_{ij}-\gamma \frac{k_i k_j}{2m}\right]\delta(c_i, c_j)\]
    where $A_{ij}$ is the adjacency matrix of the graph, $k_i$ and $k_j$ are the degrees of nodes i and j,
    m is the total number of edges in the graph, $c_i$ and $c_j$ are the communities of nodes i and j, 
    and $\delta(c_i, c_j)$ is the Kronecker delta function which is 1 if $c_i = c_j$ and 0 otherwise.
    $\gamma$ is the resolution parameter that allows us to control the size of the communities detected.
    If $\gamma$ is set to 1, we get the standard modularity. If $\gamma$ is set to a value less than 1, we will get larger communities, 
    and if $\gamma$ is set to a value greater than 1, we will get smaller communities.
    We will play around with the resolution parameter later on in the sensitivity analysis section, to see
    how it affects the community structure of our networks.
  \end{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Community Aggregation:} 
  \begin{itemize}
    \item After no more individual node movements can increase modularity, we create a 
    new network where each community is treated as a single node,
          also known as supernode.
    \item Edges between supernodes are replaced by wweighted edges,
          where the weight of the edge between two supernodes is the sum of the weights 
          of the edges between the nodes in the corresponding communities.
    \item Edges within the same community are represented as self-loops 
  \end{itemize}
\end{enumerate}

The hierarchical nature of the Louvain method allows it to capture community structure at multiple scales, and it is computationally efficient, 
with a time complexity of $O(n \log n)$ in practice, making it suitable for large networks.
However there are several notable limitations to the Louvain method, firstly, it does not guarantee that the detected communities
are internally connected. In practice, a community may consist of multiple disconnected components.
Furthermore, the algorithm may converge to a local maximum of modularity, as it only ensures that  no single node move can further increase 
modularity.
These shortcomings motivated Traag, Waltman and van Eck (2019) to introduce the Leiden algorithm, which will be discussed in the 
following section.

\subsection{Leiden Method}
We here introduce the Leiden algorithm, which guarantees well connected communities.
The algorithm is partly based on the previous introduced smart local moving algorithm, 
which is a faster version of the Louvain algorithm.
The Leiden algorithm also takes advantage of the idea of speeding up the local moving of nodes and the idea of moving nodes to 
a random neighbour by only considering a subset of nodes that are likely to move.\textcite{Leiden} states that they consider
these ideas to represent the most promising the most promising directions in which Louvain algorithm can be improved,
even though they recognise that other improvments have been suggested as well.
The Leiden algorithm consists of three phases:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Local Moving of Nodes:} 
  \begin{itemize}
    \item Similar to the Louvain method, each node is initially assigned to its own community.
    \item For each node i, we consider moving it to the community of each neighbor, and calculate 
    the change in modularity $\Delta Q$ for each possible move.
    \item The node is moved to the community that results in the highest increase in modularity, 
    but only if this move does not lead to disconnected communities.
  \end{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Refinement of Communities:} 
  \begin{itemize}
    \item After no more individual node movements can increase modularity, we refine the communities
     by splitting them into smaller subcommunities if it leads to an increase in modularity.
  \end{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Community Aggregation:} 
  \begin{itemize}
    \item Similar to the Louvain method, we create a new network where each community is treated as a single node, 
    and edges between supernodes are replaced by weighted edges.
  \end{itemize}
\end{enumerate}

\textcite{Leiden} explains that in contrast to the Louvain algorithm, 
the Leiden algorithm introduces an 
additional refinement phase between the local moving phase and the aggregation phase. 
The purpose of this refinement step is to construct a partition that refines 
the partition obtained after the local moving phase. More precisely, if the 
partition resulting from the local moving phase is denoted by $P$, the refinement 
phase identifies a new partition $P'$, where each community in $P'$ is a subset 
of a community in $P$. Thus, communities in $P$ may be subdivided into smaller, 
more internally cohesive subcommunities in $P'$.

Initially, the refined partition $P'$ assigns each node to its own singleton 
community. Subsequently, nodes are merged within their original community in $P$, 
but only if such merges improve the quality function. Importantly, merging is 
restricted to nodes that belong to the same community in $P$, ensuring that $P'$ 
remains a refinement of $P$. After the refinement phase, communities in $P'$ 
may consist of multiple nodes, yet they remain strictly contained within the 
original communities of $P$.

Compared to the Louvain algorithm, this refinement phase provides greater 
flexibility in exploring alternative partitions. Whereas Louvain performs 
greedy node movements without revisiting earlier decisions, Leiden systematically 
evaluates potential merges within communities. Merges are only accepted if they 
increase the quality function, thereby preventing deteriorations in partition quality. 
As a result, the algorithm is able to escape poorly connected or suboptimal 
community configurations.

Another important difference concerns the local moving phase itself. 
In the Louvain algorithm, nodes are considered in a fixed order, and once 
a node is moved, it is not reconsidered unless it is affected by subsequent 
movements. In contrast, the Leiden algorithm maintains a queue of nodes that 
require reevaluation. Whenever a node changes community, its neighbors are 
added to the queue to ensure that their potential moves are reconsidered. 
This dynamic updating procedure continues until no further improvements can 
be achieved. By revisiting affected nodes, Leiden avoids prematurely fixing 
suboptimal assignments and improves convergence toward higher-quality partitions.

\subsubsection{Theoretical Guarantees of the Leiden Algorithm}

The Leiden algorithm is executed iteratively, where the partition obtained 
in one iteration serves as the starting point for the next. This iterative 
structure allows the algorithm to progressively refine the community 
structure and provides several theoretical guarantees at different stages 
of convergence. Below, we give an intuitive overview of these guarantees. 
Formal definitions and mathematical proofs can be found in the supplementary material.

After each iteration of the Leiden algorithm, the following properties hold:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item All communities are $\gamma$-separated.
    \item All communities are $\gamma$-connected.
\end{enumerate}

Here, $\gamma$ denotes the resolution parameter of the quality function 
being optimized, such as modularity. 
The property of $\gamma$-separation implies that no pair of communities 
can be merged without decreasing the quality function. This property is 
also satisfied by the Louvain algorithm. 

The property of $\gamma$-connectivity is stronger than ordinary connectivity. 
It guarantees that communities are internally well connected in a manner 
consistent with the resolution parameter. In contrast, the Louvain algorithm 
does not guarantee connectivity and therefore does not ensure $\gamma$-connectivity.

An iteration in which the partition remains unchanged is referred to as a 
\emph{stable iteration}. After a stable iteration of the Leiden algorithm, 
two additional guarantees hold:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item All nodes are locally optimally assigned.
    \item All communities are subpartition $\gamma$-dense.
\end{enumerate}

Local optimality of nodes means that no individual node can be moved to 
another community in a way that improves the quality function. This 
property is also guaranteed after a stable iteration of the Louvain algorithm.

Subpartition $\gamma$-density, however, is not guaranteed by Louvain. 
A community is subpartition $\gamma$-dense if it cannot be decomposed 
into internally separable parts without reducing the quality function. 
In other words, each community consists of well-connected components 
that themselves satisfy the same density condition recursively. 
This property ensures structural cohesion beyond simple node-level optimality.

Unlike the Louvain algorithm, the Leiden algorithm may still improve 
the partition even after a stable iteration. When iterated until full 
convergence, the Leiden algorithm yields a partition satisfying two 
stronger guarantees:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item All communities are uniformly $\gamma$-dense.
    \item All communities are subset optimal.
\end{enumerate}

Uniform $\gamma$-density means that no subset of a community can be 
separated from it without decreasing the quality function. Regardless 
of how a community is divided into two parts, these parts remain 
sufficiently well connected. This implies that the overall partition 
is close to optimal with respect to the quality function.

Subset optimality represents the strongest guarantee of the Leiden algorithm. 
It states that every subset of every community is locally optimally assigned; 
that is, no subset of nodes can be moved to another community in a way that 
improves the objective function. Subset optimality implies uniform 
$\gamma$-density and all previously mentioned properties.

In summary, while the Louvain algorithm guarantees only local node-level 
optimality and $\gamma$-separation, the Leiden algorithm provides 
substantially stronger guarantees, ensuring well-connected, structurally 
cohesive, and near-optimal communities.
